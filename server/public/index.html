Berkeley Division of Data Sciences
Student Lead
Modules - In the summer of 2015, I analyzed ancient sumerian texts using modern NLP tools with another student researcher for a Near Eastern Studies professor. That professor later used our jupyter notebooks to show his students how they could use data science to enhance their understanding of ancient myths. This inspired the DS module program, which pairs a team of student researchers with a professor, who is interested in teaching their students how to use Data Science in their class. By teaching computational and analytic thinking in the context of a non-technical field, students can see how powerful data science tools can be applied to their interests. Students who have taken modules say they feel less intimidated by technical coursework and as a result often go on to take multiple technical courses. Since that summer, Modules have grown organically with student support to now impact over one thousand students per semester, making it the quickest growing educational program in Berkeley's history.
You can find many of the full lessons here at the DS Module github .

Nexus - To expand the Division's offerings and presence on campus, I connected the division of data sciences with student groups and the UC Library. Through talks over the course of a semester, we were able to begin the Data Science Nexus as a central location for students who are interested in getting involved with Data Science on campus.

Outreach - I first joined the Berkeley Data Science Education Program as a member of the Outreach team in early 2015. On the team, we worked to understand the campus culture with regards to data science as well as plan the enrollment goals for upcoming data science courses.
IBM Extreme Blue
Data Science Intern for the Watson Health Sugar.IQ mobile app
The Program - The Extreme Blue program brings together a team of 3 technical students with an MBA to design, build and present about a project sourced from IBM over a 12 week summer internship.

My Role - As the Data Science Intern, I was tasked with discovering new insights (messages that tell user's how they process blood sugar) as well as adding value however else I could find a way to. Although the team knew I'm primarily a data scientist, I was unable to access any user, patient or relevant data due to privacy laws and so I had to try to deliver value in new and creative ways. To develop new insights for users, I interviewed people with diabetes to learn about how they managed their condition, what they worried about in their daily lives and how they'd like an app to help them. After developing new insights, I developed data intake methods in java that would pull data from a user's apple healthkit and enter it into a database for analytics. I also researched and built tools in python that take in a user's data in real time and use machine learning techniques to tell the user the most likely causes of a low or high blood sugar event based on their own historic data.

The Team - In addition to building a functioning app, the team as a whole had collective responsibilities. Primarily, we had to find a way for us to innovatively add value to Watson Health. We had many heated design-thinking workshops, in which we discussed the UI/UX of the app, what we can reasonably accomplish in just 5 sprints and what we all wanted to get out of the experience. We also set weekly sprint goals, alternated as scrum master, wrote a blog post and refined a presentation that we delivered weekly to executives.
